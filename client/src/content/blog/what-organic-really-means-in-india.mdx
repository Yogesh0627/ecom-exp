"Organic" is one of the most abused words on a grocery shelf. In India it actually has a legal
definition — and once you know what backs the word, you can tell a certified product from a
marketing sticker in about five seconds.

## The two systems that matter

India runs organic certification through **NPOP** (the National Programme for Organic Production),
overseen by APEDA. Anything sold as organic through regular retail must comply with the FSSAI
organic food regulations, and there are two recognised routes:

- **NPOP / India Organic** — third-party certification, required for larger farms and anything
  exported. This is the green **"India Organic"** logo plus an accredited certification body's name.
- **PGS-India (Jaivik Bharat)** — a Participatory Guarantee System where groups of small farmers
  certify each other under a shared protocol. Lower cost, designed for smallholders selling
  locally.

Both are legitimate. The single unified consumer mark you'll see is **Jaivik Bharat**, the
government's identity for authentic organic food.

## How to actually verify it

A real certification is traceable. Look for:

1. A **certificate or scope number** you can check against the certifying body.
2. The **name of the accredited certification body** (not just the word "organic").
3. On EcoExpress, a **proof document** attached to the product — we store the scanned certificate
   so you can open it yourself.

> If a seller can't show you *who* certified the product and *under which programme*, treat the
> "organic" claim as decoration, not a guarantee.

## Why the paperwork is the product

With organic food, you are not really paying for a different-looking tomato. You're paying for a
**verifiable chain**: this farm, this soil protocol, no prohibited inputs, audited by that body.
The certificate *is* the thing you're buying. That's why every certified item in our catalogue
links to its underlying document — the trust should be inspectable, not taken on faith.

Next time you shop, don't ask "is it organic?" Ask "who says so, and can I see it?"
