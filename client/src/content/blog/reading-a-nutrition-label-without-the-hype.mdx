"Natural." "Farm fresh." "No added MSG." The front of a package is an advertisement. The **back** is
where the regulated, comparable facts live — and reading it takes less time than deciding between
two brands.

## Start with the serving size

Every number on the panel is *per serving*, and manufacturers choose the serving. A 55g biscuit
pack can quote "per 2 biscuits (14g)" so the sugar figure looks tiny. Before you read anything else:

1. Check the **serving size**.
2. Check **servings per pack**.
3. Mentally multiply if you'll eat the whole thing.

## The four numbers that matter most

Per 100g (the comparable basis — ignore per-serving when comparing two products):

- **Added sugars** — different from total sugars. Fruit's natural sugar comes with fibre; added
  sugar is empty. Watch for its many names: syrup, maltodextrin, cane juice, dextrose.
- **Sodium** — the salt proxy. Processed food is where most dietary sodium hides, not the salt shaker.
- **Saturated fat** — the fat type most worth limiting.
- **Fibre** — one of the few where *more is better*. Most people fall short.

## The traffic-light idea behind our scores

The UK's Food Standards Agency popularised a simple model: rate fat, saturated fat, sugar, and salt
as **green / amber / red** per 100g, and reward fibre, protein, and produce content. It's not
perfect, but it turns a wall of numbers into an at-a-glance judgement.

## The 30-second habit

You don't need to become a dietitian. For any packaged product, do this:

1. Serving size — is it realistic?
2. Added sugar and sodium per 100g — how do two options compare?
3. Fibre — is there any?

Do that for a week and you'll start reaching for the better option automatically. The label already
tells you the truth; you just have to read the back instead of the front.
